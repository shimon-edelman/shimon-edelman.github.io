assumptions about sampling


The likelihood function to be used depends on how the process that generated the example \(x\) relates to the true consequential region for \(C\).

A default weak assumption (Shepard, 1987) is that \(x\) and \(C\) are sampled independently, and \(x\) just happens to land inside \(C\). Tenenbaum (1997) argues that under many conditions it is more natural to treat \(x\) as a random positive example of \(C\), which involves the stronger assumption that \(x\) was explicitly sampled from \(C\).

Under weak sampling, the likelihood just measures in a binary fashion whether or not the hypothesis is consistent with the observed example: $$ p\left(h\mid x\right) = \begin{cases} 1 & \textrm{if}~x\in h \\ 0 & \textrm{otherwise} \end{cases} $$ Under strong sampling, the likelihood is more informative. Assuming \(x\) is sampled from a uniform distribution over the objects in \(h\): $$ p\left(h\mid x\right) = \begin{cases} 1/|h| & \textrm{if}~x\in h \\ 0 & \textrm{otherwise} \end{cases} $$
