NOW we are fully ready to start demystifying the mind


Certain types of dynamical systems — in particular, Turing Machines, but also brains — can implement very complex functions (input-output mappings), starting with very simple building blocks and using hierarchical abstraction.

This suggests that the mind and the brain (respectively, the most complex process and the most complex organ we know of) can be understood through hierarchical analysis of the computations and neural circuits involved.

That's where we are headed next.

how a neuron computes


The basic computation performed by a neuron:

  1. multiply each incoming signal by a weight;
  2. sum all the resulting products;
  3. compare the sum to a threshold;
  4. if the sum exceeds the threshold, then output an action potential (spike).

what each synapse computes


A useful computational view of synaptic function: a synapse acts as a weight — a number that multiplies the incoming (presynaptic) signal to compute the outgoing (postsynaptic) signal:

A crucially important detail:

The weight W and the threshold to which the sum of synaptic products is compared are modifiable by experience.

Modification can take the form of Long-Term Potentiation (LTP) or Long-Term Depression (LTD) of synaptic efficacy.

A signal arriving on the Start line stores one bit of information in the neuron by turning it ON.

The neuron subsequently remains ON due to the Feedback connection, until a Stop signal is received.

The state of the memory cell (ON or OFF) is queried by sending a signal down the Input line:

if the Output is ON, the state is ON;

if the Output is OFF, the state is OFF.

neurons and synapses: a quick summary


  • Neurons:
    • the neuron is the basic representational unit in the brain;
    • neurons are wired together in a manner that reflects the computational needs of the circuit;
    • a neuron sends signals to other neurons by emitting (all-or-none) action potentials, or "spikes", whose timing and frequency (both graded) reflect various qualities of its combined inputs.

  • Synapses:
    • the synapse is the narrow gap where a connection between neurons is made;
    • a synapse modifies the signal it transmits;
    • synaptic strength ("weight") can in turn be modified by neural activity.

  • Learning:
    • neurons learn from experience (that is, from the joint statistics of their inputs and outputs);
    • learning includes changes in synaptic weights and in the neuronal firing thresholds.

  • Complexity from simplicity:
    • Computation required for complex tasks is hierarchically decomposable into simpler elements;
    • Circuits of neurons are well-suited for supporting hierarchical computation.
